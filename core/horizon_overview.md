# Horizon Overview
### Understanding horizons as directional anchors in the REID Cognitive Architecture

The horizon is one of the four core invariants of REID.  
It represents the direction, goal, or trajectory a system, person, or
group is moving toward. The horizon gives structure to movement, meaning
to forces, and purpose to flows.

This document explains what horizons are, how they behave, and why they
matter.

---

## 1. What Horizons Are
A horizon is a **directional anchor**.

It defines:
- Where the system is going  
- What the intended outcome is  
- What “better” looks like  
- What movement should achieve  

Horizons can be:
- Short‑term  
- Long‑term  
- Narrow  
- Broad  
- Personal  
- Organizational  
- Technical  
- Emotional  

But they always provide direction.

---

## 2. Why Horizons Matter
Without a horizon:
- Forces scatter  
- Flows leak  
- State drifts  
- Systems lose coherence  
- Humans lose clarity  

With a horizon:
- Forces align  
- Flows concentrate  
- State stabilizes  
- Movement becomes meaningful  

The horizon is the **north star** of REID.

---

## 3. Horizon Properties

### **A. Direction**
The horizon points toward a future state.

### **B. Distance**
Some horizons are near; others are far.

### **C. Clarity**
A clear horizon stabilizes reasoning.  
A vague horizon increases drift.

### **D. Stability**
Horizons can be stable or shifting.  
Stable horizons reduce cognitive load.

---

## 4. Horizon Types

### **A. Explicit Horizons**
Clearly stated goals:
- “Ship the product.”  
- “Improve communication.”  
- “Stabilize the system.”  

### **B. Implicit Horizons**
Unspoken but real:
- “Avoid conflict.”  
- “Stay comfortable.”  
- “Don’t lose status.”  

### **C. Emergent Horizons**
Arise from system behavior:
- Cultural norms  
- Group dynamics  
- Organizational inertia  

REID makes implicit and emergent horizons visible.
