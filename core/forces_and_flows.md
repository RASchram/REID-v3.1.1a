REID Forces and Flows
Pressures, Incentives, Constraints, and Resource Movement
Forces and flows form the dynamic layer of the REID architecture.
While the substrate provides stability and the horizon provides direction,
the forces‑and‑flows layer models how systems actually move.

This layer captures the pressures, incentives, constraints, and resource
movements that shape real‑world behavior across any domain.

1. Purpose of the Forces‑and‑Flows Layer
This layer allows REID to reason about dynamic systems by modeling:

What pressures exist

What incentives shape behavior

What constraints limit action

How resources move through the system

Forces and flows give REID the ability to understand why systems behave
the way they do.

2. Forces (Pressures, Incentives, Constraints)
Forces represent the pressures acting on a system.
They can be internal or external, positive or negative.

Examples include:

Incentives

Deadlines

Risks

Social pressure

Market pressure

Emotional pressure

Physical constraints

Resource limitations

Forces shape the direction and intensity of system behavior.

3. Flows (Movement of Resources)
Flows represent the movement of resources through a system.

Resources may include:

Money

Energy

Time

Attention

Information

Materials

People

Compute cycles

Flows determine how a system evolves over time.

4. Interaction Between Forces and Flows
Forces influence flows, and flows influence forces.

Examples:

A deadline (force) increases the flow of effort

A shortage of resources (flow) increases pressure (force)

A new incentive (force) redirects attention (flow)

A bottleneck (flow) creates constraint pressure (force)

This interaction creates the dynamic behavior REID models.

5. Cross‑Domain Consistency
Forces and flows appear in every domain:

Technical systems

Human systems

Organizations

Families

Markets

AI reasoning

Physical environments

Because forces and flows are universal, REID can reason about any domain
using the same structural logic.

6. Forces and Flows as Predictive Tools
This layer allows REID to anticipate:

System bottlenecks

Pressure points

Likely outcomes

Resource shortages

Behavioral shifts

System failures

Optimal intervention points

It gives REID predictive capability without requiring domain‑specific rules.

7. Summary
The forces‑and‑flows layer models the dynamic behavior of systems.
It captures the pressures, incentives, constraints, and resource
movements that shape real‑world outcomes.

Together with the substrate and horizon model, this layer enables REID
to reason about complex systems with clarity, stability, and efficiency.
