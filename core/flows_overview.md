# Flows Overview
### Understanding flows as structural resources in the REID Cognitive Architecture

Flows are one of the four core invariants of REID.  
They represent the movement of resources through a system — human,
technical, organizational, or computational. Flows determine what is
possible, what is constrained, and how horizons can be reached.

This document explains what flows are, how they behave, and why they
matter.

---

## 1. What Flows Are
Flows are **resources that move** through a system.

Examples include:
- Time  
- Energy  
- Attention  
- Money  
- Compute  
- Information  
- Emotional capacity  
- Materials  
- Bandwidth  

Flows are not static.  
They move, accumulate, deplete, or get blocked.

---

## 2. Why Flows Matter
Flows determine:
- What actions are possible  
- How fast change can occur  
- Where bottlenecks form  
- How horizons can be reached  
- How forces interact with state  

Without flows, nothing moves.

---

## 3. Flow Behavior
Flows have structural properties:

- **Direction** — where the resource is moving  
- **Rate** — how fast it moves  
- **Volume** — how much is available  
- **Friction** — what slows it down  
- **Loss** — what leaks or dissipates  
- **Capacity** — how much the system can handle  

These properties apply to all domains.

---

## 4. Flow Constraints
Flows are always constrained by:
- Time limits  
- Energy limits  
- Cognitive limits  
- Compute limits  
- Budget limits  
- Emotional limits  
- Physical limits  

REID models these constraints explicitly.

---

## 5. Flow Bottlenecks
A bottleneck occurs when:
- Flow demand exceeds capacity  
- Flow is blocked  
- Flow is misallocated  
- Flow is leaking  
- Flow is diverted by stronger forces  

Bottlenecks determine the true speed of a system.
