REID Horizon Model
Goals, Timelines, and Acceptable Futures
The horizon model is the directional layer of the REID architecture.
It defines where reasoning is going, how far it extends, and what
outcomes are considered acceptable.

While the substrate provides stability, the horizon model provides
trajectory.

1. Purpose of the Horizon Model
The horizon model ensures that REID’s reasoning remains aligned with
the user’s intended outcomes over both short and long time spans.

Its core functions include:

Defining goals and desired outcomes

Establishing time horizons

Maintaining long‑arc continuity

Preventing short‑term drift

Anchoring reasoning to acceptable futures

The horizon model acts as REID’s internal compass.

2. Goal Horizons
A goal horizon defines what the system is moving toward.

Examples include:

A desired state

A completed project

A stable configuration

A long‑term plan

A multi‑human alignment point

Goal horizons give REID a clear target to reason toward.

3. Time Horizons
Time horizons define how far REID should look ahead.

They may include:

Immediate (seconds to minutes)

Short‑term (hours to days)

Mid‑term (weeks to months)

Long‑term (years to decades)

Time horizons prevent REID from over‑extending or under‑planning.

4. Acceptable Futures
Not all futures are equal.
The horizon model defines the range of outcomes that are acceptable.

This includes:

Minimum viable outcomes

Preferred outcomes

Stretch outcomes

Outcomes to avoid

Acceptable futures create a structured decision space that REID can
reason within.

5. Long‑Arc Continuity
The horizon model ensures that REID maintains coherence over long
periods of time.

This includes:

Staying aligned with long‑term goals

Avoiding short‑term noise

Maintaining direction across sessions

Preserving intent even when context changes

Long‑arc continuity is essential for multi‑step reasoning and
multi‑human coordination.

6. Interaction with the Substrate
The horizon model sits directly above the substrate.

Together they provide:

Stability (substrate)

Direction (horizon)

This combination allows REID to reason clearly, efficiently, and
consistently across domains.

7. Summary
The horizon model defines REID’s direction, goals, and acceptable
futures.
It provides the long‑arc structure that keeps reasoning aligned with
intended outcomes and prevents drift.

Without the horizon model, REID would lack direction and long‑term
coherence.
