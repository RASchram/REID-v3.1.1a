# REID for Decision‑Making
### A structural guide for everyday decisions, high‑stakes decisions, and complex multi‑factor choices using the REID Cognitive Architecture

Decision‑making is not intuition.  
Decision‑making is **structural evaluation**.

Every decision — from “What should I do today?” to “Should we move?”
to “How should we design this system?” — is shaped by the four
invariants:
- State  
- Forces  
- Horizon  
- Flows  

REID provides a universal framework for making decisions that are clear,
stable, and aligned with long‑arc direction.

This document explains how to apply REID to decision‑making.

---

# 1. Why Decision‑Making Needs REID

People struggle with decisions because of:
- Overwhelm  
- Emotional noise  
- Conflicting priorities  
- Unclear goals  
- Limited resources  
- Cognitive overload  
- Fear of regret  
- Horizon drift  

REID solves these structurally by modeling:
- Where you are (state)  
- What’s pushing on you (forces)  
- Where you’re going (horizon)  
- What you have to work with (flows)  

Decisions become structural, not emotional.

---

# 2. The REID Decision Loop

All decisions follow the same structural cycle:

```
1. Identify State
2. Map Forces
3. Clarify Horizon
4. Check Flows
5. Evaluate Options
6. Choose the Path with the Least Drift
```

This loop works for:
- Daily decisions  
- High‑stakes decisions  
- Technical decisions  
- Emotional decisions  
- Family decisions  
- Organizational decisions  

---

# 3. Step 1 — Identify State

State determines:
- What is possible  
- What is realistic  
- What is sustainable  

Examples:
- “I’m exhausted.”  
- “The system is unstable.”  
- “The team is overloaded.”  
- “The family is stressed.”  

State is the ground truth.

---

# 4. Step 2 — Map Forces

Forces shape the decision environment.

Examples:
- Deadlines  
- Expectations  
- Emotional pressure  
- Market conditions  
- Family needs  
- Technical constraints  

Forces reveal the real pressures behind the decision.

---

# 5. Step 3 — Clarify Horizon

Horizon determines:
- Direction  
- Priority  
- Meaning  
- Alignment  

Examples:
- “We want stability.”  
- “I want long‑term health.”  
- “We want to reduce complexity.”  
- “I want to grow creatively.”  

A clear horizon simplifies decisions.

---

# 6. Step 4 — Check Flows

Flows determine:
- Capacity  
- Feasibility  
- Sustainability  

Examples:
- Time  
- Energy  
- Money  
- Emotional bandwidth  
- Compute  
- Attention  

Flow scarcity is one of the biggest decision constraints.

---

# 7. Step 5 — Evaluate Options Structurally

Each option is evaluated by asking:

**How does this option affect:**
- State?  
- Forces?  
- Horizon?  
- Flows?  

The best option is the one that:
- Reduces drift  
- Stabilizes state  
- Aligns forces  
- Protects flows  
- Supports the horizon  

This removes emotional noise.

---

# 8. Step 6 — Choose the Path with the Least Drift

Drift is the enemy of good decisions.

The best decision is the one that:
- Moves you toward the horizon  
- Minimizes force conflict  
- Preserves flows  
- Stabilizes state  

This is the REID definition of “good.”
