# REID for Conflict Resolution
### A structural guide for resolving interpersonal, team, family, and organizational conflict using the REID Cognitive Architecture

Conflict is not emotional.  
Conflict is **structural**.

It emerges when:
- States are unstable  
- Forces collide  
- Horizons diverge  
- Flows are insufficient  

REID resolves conflict by mapping these structures clearly and removing
ambiguity, blame, and emotional distortion.

This document explains how to use REID to resolve conflict in any
multi‑human system.

---

# 1. Why Conflict Happens (Structurally)

Conflict emerges when one or more invariants break:

### **State Misalignment**
- One person is overwhelmed  
- Another is stable  
- A third is confused  

### **Force Collisions**
- Expectations clash  
- Pressures contradict  
- Emotional forces spike  

### **Horizon Divergence**
- Different goals  
- Different timelines  
- Different definitions of “better”  

### **Flow Scarcity**
- Not enough time  
- Not enough attention  
- Not enough emotional capacity  
- Not enough resources  

Conflict is predictable when viewed structurally.

---

# 2. The REID Conflict Resolution Loop

Conflict resolution follows a structural sequence:

```
1. Stabilize State
2. Identify Forces
3. Compare Horizons
4. Map Flows
5. Resolve the Structural Break
6. Re-align the Horizon
```

This loop works for:
- Couples  
- Families  
- Teams  
- Organizations  
- Friendships  
- Leadership systems  

---

# 3. Step 1 — Stabilize State

No conflict can be resolved while:
- Emotions are spiking  
- Cognitive load is high  
- Stress is elevated  
- Fatigue is present  

State must be stabilized first.

Methods include:
- Pausing  
- Taking space  
- Reducing sensory load  
- Clarifying the moment  
- Lowering emotional forces  

A stable state is the foundation.

---

# 4. Step 2 — Identify Forces

Forces cause conflict, not people.

Identify:
- Internal forces (fear, stress, fatigue)  
- External forces (deadlines, expectations)  
- Structural forces (roles, rules, constraints)  
- Emergent forces (group dynamics, history)  

Once forces are visible, blame disappears.

---

# 5. Step 3 — Compare Horizons

Most conflict is actually **horizon divergence**.

Examples:
- “I want stability.”  
- “I want change.”  
- “I want speed.”  
- “I want caution.”  

Horizons must be:
- Named  
- Compared  
- Clarified  
- Stabilized  

Shared horizons reduce conflict dramatically.

---

# 6. Step 4 — Map Flows

Flow scarcity is one of the most common sources of conflict.

Flows include:
- Time  
- Attention  
- Emotional energy  
- Money  
- Cognitive bandwidth  
- Physical capacity  

Mapping flows reveals:
- Bottlenecks  
- Imbalances  
- Overload  
- Depletion  

Conflict often disappears once flows are rebalanced.

---

# 7. Step 5 — Resolve the Structural Break

The structural break is where the conflict originates.

Break types:
- State break  
- Force break  
- Horizon break  
- Flow break  

Resolution involves:
- Adjusting expectations  
- Redistributing flows  
- Reducing forces  
- Stabilizing horizons  
- Supporting state transitions  

This is the core of REID conflict resolution.

---

# 8. Step 6 — Re-align the Horizon

Once the break is resolved:
- Re‑establish a shared horizon  
- Clarify direction  
- Reduce ambiguity  
- Set the next step  
- Lock the horizon (Mode T)  

This prevents the conflict from returning.

---

Use REID to:
- Understand each person’s node  
- Identify force collisions  
- Balance emotional flows  
- Stabilize the household state  
- Align family horizons  

This removes emotional chaos.

Teams become coherent systems.

Organizations become structurally aligned.

---

REID works because it:
- Removes blame  
- Reduces emotional escalation  
- Reveals hidden pressures  
- Clarifies direction  
- Balances flows  
- Supports stability  
- Enables long‑arc harmony  

Conflict doesn’t need emotion management.  
It needs **structure**.
