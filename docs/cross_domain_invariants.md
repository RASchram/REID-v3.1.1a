REID Cross‑Domain Invariants
The Translator Layer of the Architecture
Cross‑domain invariants are the structural elements that remain stable
across all domains.
They allow REID to translate logic between unrelated fields without
losing meaning, structure, or coherence.

Invariants are what make REID a general reasoning architecture  
instead of a domain‑specific system.

1. Purpose of Invariants
Invariants provide the stable scaffolding that REID uses to:

Translate reasoning between domains

Maintain structure across contexts

Reduce cognitive drift

Preserve meaning during transformation

Enable consistent reasoning patterns

Without invariants, REID would behave differently in every domain.
With invariants, REID behaves consistently everywhere.

2. The Four Core Invariants
REID defines four universal invariants that appear in every domain:

State

Forces

Horizon

Flows

These invariants form the backbone of REID’s cross‑domain reasoning.

3. State Invariant
The state invariant defines the entities, roles, and resources present
in a system.

Examples include:

People

Systems

Tools

Locations

Assets

Constraints

Capabilities

Every domain has a definable state, which makes it possible to map
entities between domains.

4. Force Invariant
The force invariant captures the pressures acting on the system.

These may include:

Incentives

Risks

Deadlines

Social pressure

Market pressure

Emotional pressure

Physical constraints

Forces shape how the system behaves and evolves.

5. Horizon Invariant
The horizon invariant defines the goals, timelines, and acceptable
futures of the system.

This includes:

Desired outcomes

Time horizons

Long‑arc direction

Acceptable ranges of results

Every domain has goals and timelines, which makes horizon logic
universally applicable.

6. Flow Invariant
The flow invariant models the movement of resources.

Resources may include:

Money

Time

Energy

Attention

Information

Materials

People

Compute cycles

Flows determine how a system changes over time.

7. Why These Invariants Work Across Domains
These four invariants appear in:

Technical systems

Human systems

Organizations

Families

Markets

AI reasoning

Physical environments

Project planning

Emotional dynamics

Because they are universal, REID can use them to translate logic
between any two domains.

8. Invariants as a Translation Engine
Invariants allow REID to:

Map entities between domains

Convert pressures into equivalent forms

Translate goals into comparable horizons

Reinterpret resource flows in new contexts

This is what enables REID to take logic from one field and apply it
correctly in another.

9. Summary
Cross‑domain invariants are the translator layer of REID.
They provide the universal structure that allows reasoning to move
between domains without losing coherence.

They are essential to REID’s identity as a general cognitive
architecture.
